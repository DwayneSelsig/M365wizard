## The big one: Study and Learn

The new **Study and Learn Agent** is the highlight. Students can use their own content to create quizzes, flashcards, matching games, and fill-in-the-blank exercises. It is designed to coach rather than simply give the answer—useful for brainstorming an essay or practising a difficult subject.

For K-12 students, AI features need to be enabled by IT and are intended for ages 13 and over. Check your tenant settings before promising this to students. Microsoft has a short [Study and Learn training module](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/training/modules/develop-understanding-study-learn-agent-copilot-app/) if you want to try it out.

## More useful tools, in more places

The same learning activities now show up in the Learning Activities app, Teach, Teams, Copilot notebooks, and Study and Learn. That is good news: a teacher can create practice material once and share it where students already work.

Teachers also get more in **Teach**, including lesson-plan drafts, rubrics, quizzes, standards alignment, and ways to adapt existing content to another reading level. Teams Classwork already includes AI creation tools, while new OneNote and Windows experiences are on the way.

## My take

Don't try to roll out everything at once. Start with one class and one simple use case—revision flashcards, for example. Let teachers check the output before sharing it, and let IT verify access and age settings. Microsoft documents the current availability and controls in its [Education AI feature guidance](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-365/education/guide/1-reference/manage-microsoft-365-education-ai-features).

The video also covers Learning Zone, LMS integration, and new Teams assignment options. Several of those features are still preview or planned for later in 2026, so check the current status before planning around them. For the broader context, see our [Copilot guide](/docs/services/copilot).
