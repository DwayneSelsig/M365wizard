## More tools, broader access

Microsoft has announced a wide range of new tools for education. Not all of them are completely new, but they make existing Microsoft 365 apps smarter and better connected. What stands out most to me is that these AI features will be available with A1, A3, and A5 licenses at no additional cost.

## Learning support that guides students

I'm especially interested in the Study and Learn Agent and Copilot Notebooks. Instead of simply giving students an answer, the Study and Learn Agent guides them through the learning process step by step, which could make it a great tool for personalized learning. Copilot Notebooks also looks useful for bringing information from your own documents and sources together in one place and turning it into focused study material.

## The opportunity for more active learning

I'm excited about the possibilities, particularly the opportunity for students to practice at their own level, get support when they need it, and engage more actively with the material. Still, great education ultimately depends on people making thoughtful choices: educators, students, administrators, and Microsoft itself.

## Support needs to keep up

The technology also needs to be easy to support, not just easy to use. With so many new features, internal support requests could involve a lot of investigation. Troubleshooting AI does not always follow a clear, repeatable checklist, which can make things especially challenging for functional administrators.

## Responsible use is the next challenge

Now that access is becoming less of a barrier, the bigger challenge will be using these tools responsibly. That also means making sure the technology behind the scenes remains understandable, manageable, and supportable.
